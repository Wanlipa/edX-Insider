There's a neat framework for thinking about how students learn called ICAP. The gist of ICAP is that there are four ways of delivering information:

  • Passive: For example, listening to a lecture. Watching a video. Quietly reading a book.
  • Active: Manipulating concepts. For example, underlining text in a book, or working through a problem applying information just learned.
  • Constructive: Figuring things out. Creating new information. For example, deriving a key theorem, or writing a paper.
  • Interactive: Learning through peer interactions.

The terms used here mean very different things to different people. In particular, constructive learning has very different meanings in different research communities. For example, many members in educational philosophy use it interchangeably with constructivist learning, which is at best weakly related to the definition from ICAP (which comes from a cognitive science community). Likewise, active learning often embodies the full spectrum. Just to have a common language, throughout this unit, we'll use the language of ICAP (and use constructivist when refering to the use from educational psychology).

Piotr originally designed the platform with the goal of providing affordances to make it easy to move from passive learning to active, and then to constructive (although not yet to interactive). Most courses on edX have gone as far as the first step, to active, with simple embedded "finger exercises". We'll explore a bit about these first three of the four modes, and in particular, how to use technology to move towards constructive learning. Here's a paper explaining these concepts in more detail if you are interested. The paper strongly suggests that moving from passive to active, and again from active to constructive shows large learning gains (although there are researchers who credibly disagree with the latter assertion). A longer-term goal of the platform is to also enable interactive (which is only sometime better than constructive), but we're not there yet.
